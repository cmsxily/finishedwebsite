Mental Health Awareness

It is time to change how we think and speak about mental illness.

What is mental health?

Mental health is something we all have. It includes your thoughts, feelings about yourself and others, how we relate to other people, how we function day to day and how we cope with the ups and downs of life. Having good mental health it is not about having no mental health problems, it's about feeling good enough about ourselves. Being able to do the things that matter to us and having the resilience to deal with the difficult situations we all experience in life.

Mental illness can come in many froms. The American Psychiatric Association defines mental illness as a health condition involving changes in thinking, emotion or behavior. These conditions may affect someone's ability to relate to others and function each day. Mental illness may not be visible on the outside, it doesn't mean somene isn't hurting on the inside.

Mental health can be complex because our experience of it is personal and unique. What we know that the more we normalize conversations about our mental health, the easier it will be for young people and adults to seek the support they need when they most need it.

When talking about mental health it can be useful for us to think about it like a scale. A mental health scale that goes from poor to good mental health, depending on what's going on our lives, and the support we have around us.

poor good
isolation having healthy relationships
low self-esteem high self-esteem
lack of personal hygiene satisfied with life
fatigue not easily overwhelmed by emotion
risky behavior resilient
running away from responsibilities has sense of responsibility
Mental health does not mean mental illness.

Why is mental health important?

psycho, weirdo, nuts, schizoid, nut case, bonkers, wacko, maniac, lunatic, looney, suicidal

We may not realise it but using these words to describe someone with a mental health condition can be very harmful.

Every 15 minutes someone dies from commiting suicide. That's 38,000 suicides each year. That's twice as many people that die by traffic fatality every year. 90% of suicides are related to mental health illneses. Global mental health is an area that's a little bit behind. The world health organization defines "health" as a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being. Mental well-being is part of the definition of health. Why don't we talk about this?

Mental health is frecuently stigmitized around the world and some cultures don't even have words to describe mental health illneses. On top of that, it can be a difficult topic to discuss.

Mental health and well-being go beyond neurological health. Depression is a risk factor for developing chronic diseases like hypertension, cardiovascular disease and diabetes.

It is important for us to all work together, to incorporate mental health as part of our overall health profile.

What causes mental health to be affected?

Mental health can be affected by many things like:

  • childhood abuse, trauma, or neglect
  • social isolation
  • experiencing discrimination and stigma
  • social disadvantage, poverty or debt
  • losing someone close to you
  • sereve or long-term stress
  • having long-term physical health condition
  • unemployment or losing your job
  • homelessness or poor housing
  • drug or alcohol misuse
  • domestic violence, bullying or other abuse
  • trauma
  • physical causes
    • The absence of mental illness does not necessarily mean that someone is mentally healthy.

    Mental health wellness tips:

    Focus on the positive-think about the good things in your life, focus on solutions instead of the negatives (problems).

    Practice gratitude -each day recognize the things you are grateful for.

    Connect with others-being around friends and family can improve your mood and self-esteem. Meaningful relationships helps mantain a positive attitude which gives you space for personal growth.

    Mantain physical health-have a healthy diet, exercise a litte each day, and get enough sleep. You will feel better after a workout even if it's just a walk around the block.

    Develop strong coping skills-learn to respond in a healty way to stress, sadness, criticism, and negativity. One way of coping can be writing your negative experiences and how they made you feel.

    Get professional help-learn how to recognize the signs and symptoms that are a warning that you are not doing so well. It's better to reach out sooner rather than later. Asking for help takes courage, it does not mean you are weak. A health professional can help you in ways your friends and family can't.

    It can be difficult to apply all of these onto your life, take your time and try them one by one. Some may help you, some may not because we are all different.

    There is no health without mental health.
